Key Risks
Risk 1
high threat
Risk 1 — Arsenal's record-equalling corner threat against a porous defence. Arsenal have scored 16 goals from corners this season, equalling the all-time Premier League record, with routines orchestrated by set-piece coach Nicolas Jover. Gabriel Magalhães, William Saliba, Viktor Gyökeres, and Kai Havertz are all aerial targets from Declan Rice's elite delivery. Newcastle have conceded 45 goals this season — the seventh-most in the league — and without Schär and Botman available for much of the run-in, the aerial presence to combat Arsenal's rehearsed corner routines is significantly diminished. Every corner Arsenal win is a genuine scoring opportunity, and Newcastle's defensive record suggests they will concede plenty of them.
Risk 2
medium threat
Risk 2 — Arsenal's counter-press neutralises Newcastle's only reliable attacking weapon. Newcastle's most effective tool all season has been their aggressive man-to-man pressing system to win the ball back high and transition quickly. But Arsenal are specifically designed to counter this: their structured counter-pressing system immediately swarms the ball upon losing possession, denying opponents the sustained possession needed to build attacks. In the reverse fixture at St James' Park, Arsenal's high pressing forced Newcastle into errors and their final-third output was negligible. Without Bruno Guimarães to retain the ball under pressure and dictate tempo through the middle, Newcastle's midfield will likely be overrun by the Rice-Zubimendi pivot.
Risk 3
medium threat
Risk 3 — Arsenal's positional play in the 3-2-5 will overload Newcastle's man-marking. Arsenal's system morphs into a 3-2-5 shape in possession with an inverted fullback tucking into midfield to create central numerical superiority. Newcastle's man-to-man pressing relies on every player winning their individual duel — but the 3-2-5 creates free men by design, as the positional rotations between Eze, Ødegaard (if fit), Saka, and Gyökeres drag markers out of their zones. When Newcastle's press is bypassed, the high defensive line is left catastrophically exposed, as the 2-7 Barcelona second leg demonstrated in its most extreme form. Arsenal's patient build-up from the back, with David Raya initiating short passing sequences, is purpose-built to draw the press and then play through it.
Where the Matchup Favours Newcastle
1
Transition speed can punish Arsenal's high line
Arsenal's high defensive line has been identified by multiple analysts as vulnerable to pace played in behind — teams that can bypass the press and launch quick direct balls over the top have found joy. Newcastle's Anthony Gordon provides exactly the kind of pace and directness on the counter that can exploit the space between Arsenal's last line and David Raya. When Newcastle's man-to-man press does work, it wins the ball in dangerous areas and creates opportunities to attack before the defence resets. Harvey Barnes and Anthony Elanga add further speed to the forward line. The key is making these transitions count — Newcastle average only 1.4 goals per game, so clinical finishing in the rare moments Arsenal's structure breaks will be essential. If Howe sets up to absorb possession and strike on the break rather than going toe-to-toe, these transition moments could be Newcastle's primary route to goal.
2
Ødegaard's absence dulls Arsenal's creative edge
Martin Ødegaard has been Arsenal's primary creative hub all season, and his knee injury since late February has visibly reduced the team's fluency in the final third. While he was back in training as of March 20, his fitness for this fixture remains uncertain — and even if he starts, match sharpness after weeks out is a legitimate concern. Without Ødegaard's ability to find pockets of space between the lines and thread final-third passes, Arsenal's build-up becomes more predictable and reliant on wide progressions through Saka and Madueke. Newcastle's midfield trio of Joelinton, Jacob Ramsey, and whoever deputises for Bruno Guimarães can focus on compressing central space and forcing Arsenal wide, where their crossing game is less lethal than their combinative play through the middle. This is a narrow but real window — Newcastle must exploit it before Arteta's squad depth papers over the absence.
3
Dan Burn's aerial presence versus zonal corners
Arsenal defend set pieces with a zonal marking system rather than man-marking, and while their overall defensive record is strong, zonal systems can be vulnerable to attackers who aggressively attack specific zones with physical presence. Dan Burn, at 6'7", is Newcastle's primary aerial weapon and a genuine mismatch against any zonal scheme — he can target the zone rather than being tracked by a designated marker. Fabian Schär, if fit after the international break, adds another aerial threat. Newcastle have scored approximately eight goals from set pieces this season, proving they can be dangerous from dead-ball situations. Against a side defending zonally, Howe should look to maximise corner and free-kick opportunities, using Burn as the focal point of near-post or back-post runs designed to attack the seams between Arsenal's designated zones. This may be Newcastle's most reliable route to a goal given their open-play struggles against organised defences.
What to Watch For
Whether Howe sets up in his 4-2-3-1 defensive shape rather than the standard 4-3-3 — the extra midfielder behind the ball would signal a deliberate strategy to absorb Arsenal's possession and protect the transition spaces that have been so devastatingly exploited this season.
Watch how Newcastle's centre-backs behave in the first 15 minutes. If they step high into midfield to engage Arsenal's dropping forwards — their usual aggressive approach — it will leave vast spaces behind for Gyökeres and Saka to exploit. If they sit deeper and let Arsenal have the ball in front of them, Howe has acknowledged the transition problem.
Track Tino Livramento's positioning at right-back if he's fit. In Newcastle's pressing system, Livramento jumps to mark opposition full-backs — but against Arsenal's inverted fullback structure, this could leave him stranded in no-man's-land while Arsenal's 3-2-5 creates overloads centrally.
