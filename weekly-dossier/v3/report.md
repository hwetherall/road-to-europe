## 5. Schär and Krafth Out: How Newcastle's Defensive Absences Reshape the Palace Trip

## Newcastle United: Injury Picture and Tactical Outlook

Two confirmed absences define Newcastle's defensive options heading into Selhurst Park. Fabian Schär has been sidelined since early January with a broken ankle, and Emil Krafth is out for the season following knee surgery in February. Both were depth options, but Schär's loss is the more tactically significant — he is Newcastle's most comfortable ball-playing centre-back, capable of stepping into midfield lines and driving transitions from deep.

With Schär absent, the likely pairing at centre-back falls to Sven Botman and Malvem Thiaw. Botman is a composed, physical defender best used in a settled defensive structure; Thiaw offers athleticism and recovery pace but is still developing his reading of high-defensive-line situations. The unit is capable but less comfortable initiating through the thirds, which puts extra demand on Bruno Guimarães and Sandro Tonali to recycle possession efficiently and prevent Newcastle from becoming one-dimensional.

The midfield engine remains Newcastle's clearest strength. Tonali and Guimarães — each rated among the strongest players in the squad — form a double pivot with genuine defensive competence and the passing range to shift the tempo. Joelinton's role as a physical third midfielder allows the shape to compact quickly in transition, which will matter against a Palace side that looks to counter through Ismaïla Sarr's pace from wide areas.

In the final third, Anthony Gordon on the left is Newcastle's most decisive attacker — high pace, direct, and the primary threat in behind. With Krafth unavailable at right-back, Tino Livramento is the expected starter in that role; his athleticism helps him recover, but he is an attack-minded profile rather than a defensive anchor, so Palace may target that flank. Jacob Murphy and Anthony Elanga provide width on the opposite side, giving Newcastle rotational options without forcing a tactical rethink.

The striker picture is worth watching. Yoane Wissa leads the line, with Nick Woltemade offering a more hold-up-oriented alternative off the bench. Neither is a finished product at this level over a full season, which means Newcastle will rely on overloads from midfield runners — particularly Joelinton — to generate volume in the final third.

Overall, Newcastle carry a clear squad edge over Crystal Palace by starting XI quality and by depth. The defensive restructuring enforced by injuries does not fundamentally break the shape, but it does narrow the margin for error against a Palace attack built around set-piece threat and direct wide play.

## 6. Crystal Palace vs Newcastle: Away Favourites, Defensive Gamble, +5.8pp on the Line

## Crystal Palace vs Newcastle United — Match Preview

Newcastle travel to Selhurst Park on Sunday as the away favourite, carrying the round's single most valuable result in their own hands. A win here is worth **+5.8pp** on its own — more than half of what a flawless perfect weekend would deliver.

### Opportunities

Crystal Palace's defensive structure invites pressure from energetic midfields. Adam Wharton anchors their centre, but Palace's wide-mid pairing — Yeremy Pino and Ismaïla Sarr — commit forward aggressively, which opens the channels Newcastle like to exploit on the counter. Anthony Gordon's pace off the left is the most direct route to chaos behind Palace's fullbacks, and with Tino Livramento getting forward from right-back, Newcastle can generate width quickly on both sides.

The Bruno Guimarães–Tonali double pivot is the quality engine that drives Newcastle's transitions. Against a Palace midfield that tends to press with Wharton sitting and Donyell Malen-esque runners ahead, there is space to be found between the lines — exactly the zone Joelinton targets when he arrives late from deep. If Newcastle can sustain midfield dominance for stretches, Palace's shape will be stretched.

Palace's centre-back pairing of Marc Guéhi and Maxence Lacroix is solid, but Guéhi's recovery pace is limited in behind — Lacroix is the faster of the two. Yoane Wissa running directly at Guéhi in transition, or Woltemade offering hold-up play to turn him, are both viable routes to creating chances.

### Risks

The defensive reshuffle — covered in full above — narrows the margin for error. With Botman and Thiaw expected to partner at centre-back, Newcastle carry less composure when building from deep under pressure. Palace's set-piece game and Jean-Philippe Mateta's physical presence in the box are precisely the conditions that test a patched back line most severely. Mateta thrives on aerial duels and penalty-area battles; neither Botman nor Thiaw has the ball-playing instinct to defuse pressure by simply playing out.

Selhurst Park's atmosphere amplifies Palace's counter-press, and Thomas Frank's — apologies, Oliver Glasner's — Palace structure is built to exploit moments of Newcastle ball-retention failure. Sarr's burst of pace from wide positions is a direct threat to Livramento in one-on-one situations; an attack-minded right-back against a 91-rated pace winger is an uncomfortable matchup that Palace will target deliberately.

The fixture is winnable — the odds and the squad quality both point Newcastle's way — but it demands a clean defensive performance that the current injury context makes harder to guarantee. With 7 rounds remaining, this is not the time for clean-sheet margins to slip.
