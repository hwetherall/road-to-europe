But from set pieces, they're more vulnerable than their overall defensive record suggests. Newcastle, meanwhile, are one of the most dangerous set-piece teams in the league. Dan Burn, Sven Botman, and Fabian Schär all provide aerial targets that can cause problems in the box. This is a genuine statistical mismatch: Newcastle's set-piece attack against Arsenal's relative set-piece vulnerability.

**2. Arsenal's left side is less protected than their right.**

Arsenal's attacking identity runs through the right — the Saka-Ødegaard-White triangle is the most productive combination in the Premier League. When Arsenal attack, their right side is meticulously structured with overlapping and underlapping runners. But their left side is more variable. Depending on whether Arteta selects an inverting full-back (Timber/Zinchenko-type) or an overlapping one, the balance shifts — and the transition back to defence on that flank can be slower.

Newcastle's Anthony Gordon (5 goals, 2 assists this season) operates primarily on the left wing but often drifts centrally. If Newcastle can win the ball and transition quickly down Gordon's side, they may find space before Arsenal's left-side defensive structure resets. Arsenal's tactical analysis this season shows they prioritise controlling transitions through a "3+2 rest defence" — but when that structure breaks, even momentarily, they're exposed.

**3. Arsenal's achilles heel: the final 15 minutes at home.**

Arsenal's home record is dominant, but there's a pattern in their underlying numbers. Their control tends to be most complete in the first 30 minutes and between minutes 45-75. Late in matches, particularly when the score is tight, Arsenal's press intensity drops and opponents gain territory. Three of Arsenal's drawn home matches this season have seen late equalisers or periods of sustained opposition pressure. They don't collapse — but they bend.

Newcastle's fitness levels and bench options could matter here. Eddie Howe has rotated more aggressively than most managers this season, partly because of Champions League commitments. If Newcastle can stay within a goal at the Emirates through 70 minutes, they have the legs and the substitute quality (Harvey Barnes, Jacob Ramsey, Elanga from the bench) to mount a serious late push.

**Gyökeres in transition.** Arsenal's summer signing has scored prolifically this season and is lethal on the counter. If Newcastle push forward and leave space in behind, a single Saka-to-Gyökeres ball could end the contest. **Saka on the ball.** Bukayo Saka has been Arsenal's most important player all season. He pins full-backs, draws fouls, and creates from nothing. Lewis Hall or Valentino Livramento will need to be disciplined — not diving in, not getting turned. The goal is to make Saka beat you twice before he can deliver.

### What to Watch For

If Newcastle are going to get a result, the signs will be visible early. Watch for:

- **Newcastle winning the corner count in the first half.** If they're generating set pieces, their aerial advantage is in play.
- **Arsenal's full-back positioning.** If the left-back is inverting (tucking inside), Newcastle's wide right can be exploited on transition. If he's overlapping, the space is behind him instead.
- **How Newcastle handle the first 15 minutes.** Arsenal typically come out with high-intensity pressing at home. If Newcastle survive that spell without conceding, the match opens up. If they concede early, the game plan changes completely.
