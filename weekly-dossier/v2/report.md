## 5. Howe's Hand Forced: Newcastle's Injury Toll and the Shape of What's Left

## Howe's Hand Forced: Newcastle's Injury Toll and the Shape of What's Left

Newcastle head to Selhurst Park with their squad meaningfully diminished. Callum Wilson is out for eight weeks — a blow that removes the focal point of Eddie Howe's attacking shape and leaves Newcastle without a natural target striker to hold the line and bring wide runners into play. Nick Pope is being assessed by a specialist, and his availability for this fixture is genuinely uncertain. Joe Willock is also flagged as a concern, trimming midfield depth at a moment when rotation options matter.

The Wilson absence is the most consequential. Newcastle's press-and-transition game relies on a striker who can pin centre-backs and win flick-ons; without him, Howe must either shift Yves Wissa into the central striker role — where his pace rather than physicality drives the threat — or deploy a deeper shape that sacrifices vertical directness. Wissa is a credible alternative given his movement and finishing instincts, but the dynamic changes: Palace's centre-backs won't face the same aerial contest, and Newcastle lose a reference point for their second-ball game.

With Willock doubtful, the midfield picture tightens around the two players who have defined Newcastle's engine room this season. Bruno Guimarães and Sandro Tonali are the core of everything — progressive passing, pressing triggers, physicality in transition. Both are fit and expected to start, and when both play well Newcastle have the midfield quality to control matches at Championship and European level. The concern is what sits behind them: Lewis Miley is talented but raw, and the depth drop from the first pair to the alternatives is steep.

At the back, Pope's uncertainty is significant because his replacement, Aaron Ramsdale, is a capable Premier League goalkeeper but represents a step down in Howe's preferred system — Pope's sweeping and command of the high line have been integral to Newcastle's defensive structure. If Ramsdale starts at Selhurst, the defensive line may sit fractionally deeper.

Crystal Palace also have an injury of note — their £35m star suffered a training-ground knock in the days before this fixture, though specific confirmation of that player's identity and availability at kick-off has not been confirmed. It could alter Palace's attacking threat, which centres on Jean-Philippe Mateta as the focal point and Ismaïla Sarr's pace in behind.

On paper, Newcastle's starting XI still carries a quality advantage over Palace's, and the midfield axis of Guimarães and Tonali gives Howe a platform to dominate possession. The question is whether the absences create enough structural fragility — particularly the lack of a dominant striker and a possibly weakened goalkeeper — for Palace to exploit on their own ground. Anthony Gordon's pace on the left and Anthony Elanga's directness on the right give Newcastle the tools to hurt Palace in transition even without Wilson; the shape just requires more movement to compensate for the loss of the focal point his presence creates.

## 6. Crystal Palace vs Newcastle: Selhurst Park, Maximum Stakes

## Match Focus: Crystal Palace vs Newcastle United

**Matchday 32 | Selhurst Park | Sunday, 12 April**

As established, a Newcastle win here is worth **+5.8pp** to their top-7 odds — the single most leveraged result of the entire round. Nothing else on the card comes close. This is the game.

---

### Opportunity

Crystal Palace's squad profile has a clear ceiling. Jean-Philippe Mateta is their attacking focal point — a physical, penalty-area striker who thrives on service from wide areas — but the quality drop from their best XI to the depth behind it is significant. Adam Wharton is a composed defensive midfielder with genuine talent, though at 21 he can be pressed into errors under sustained intensity. Ismaïla Sarr offers pace in behind and will be the primary Palace threat on the break.

Newcastle's midfield engine changes the dynamic. With Tonali and Guimarães both fit and expected to start, Howe has the tools to control the territory between the lines — both players press with structure, recycle quickly, and give the wide attackers platform to operate. Anthony Gordon and Anthony Elanga are the mechanism for the Magpies' best attacking moments: Gordon's movement on the left and Elanga's directness on the right can isolate Palace's full-backs, and Selhurst's tight pitch actually suits a side that attacks in quick combinations rather than through a dominant aerial striker.

Reports indicate Crystal Palace have an injury concern of their own entering this match — a key player suffered a training-ground knock days before kick-off. If that disrupts Palace's attacking structure, Newcastle's defensive organisation may face fewer of the combinations that made Palace difficult to handle earlier in the season.

With 7 rounds remaining, this is the fixture where Newcastle can genuinely reshape their European conversation rather than just manage it.

---

### Risk

The injury picture, covered above, bites hardest in two positions. Without a dominant centre-forward, Yoan Wissa leads the line — a clever, mobile striker, but not a player who pins centre-backs and wins the second-ball battles that Callum Wilson would generate. Marc Guéhi and Maxence Lacroix are a composed, physically capable centre-back pairing; they will not be troubled aerially, and the lack of a physical focal point gives them a more comfortable evening than Wilson would have provided.

At the other end, the goalkeeper situation introduces real uncertainty. If Pope does not pass his specialist assessment, Aaron Ramsdale starts between the sticks. Ramsdale is Premier League quality, but Selhurst Park is a test of nerve — the crowd, the long balls in behind, and Mateta's physicality on set-pieces all become sharper challenges without Pope's command of the high line and his sweeping instincts.

Palace are also well-organised under their system, and Selhurst's atmosphere has buried more clinical away sides than Newcastle. Howe's depleted squad carries structural vulnerabilities — midfield depth behind the first pair is thin, and one yellow card to either Tonali or Guimarães changes the shape of the game. Mateta remains a constant aerial threat, and if Palace can force Newcastle wide and win the physical duels in the middle, the away side's lack of a target striker becomes a structural problem rather than just an inconvenience.

The market reflects a genuine contest. Newcastle are favoured, but this is Selhurst Park, and the Magpies are travelling with a patched squad. The upside if they win is enormous. The cost of dropping points here, with 7 rounds remaining and the gap to close above them, is just as significant.
