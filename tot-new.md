Methodology
Monte Carlo simulation of 10,000 season outcomes. Match probabilities sourced from bookmaker odds where available, and estimated from Elo ratings (derived from points-per-game) with home advantage adjustment for remaining fixtures. Each simulation randomly resolves all remaining matches using Poisson-distributed goal sampling, calculates final standings, and records finishing positions.

Sensitivity analysislocks each fixture to every possible result (home win / draw / away win) and re-runs 1,000 simulations per lock to measure the impact on the selected team's qualification odds.

What-If modelets you manually lock fixture outcomes and see how they affect the selected team's odds in real time. Lock any fixture — not just the selected team's — since a rival's loss can matter more than your team's win.

Scenarios stack multiple what-if assumptions (fixture locks and probability modifiers) to explore compound effects on qualification odds. Use the chat sidebar to describe scenarios in natural language.

Tottenham's season has been a slow-motion catastrophe that accelerated into freefall. A managerial sacking after just eight months led to Igor Tudor's appointment as interim in February, but the former Juventus coach has managed just one point from his first five Premier League matches — a 1-1 draw at Liverpool sandwiched between home drubbings from Arsenal (1-4), Crystal Palace (1-3), and Nottingham Forest (0-3). The injury crisis has been relentless: at one point up to 13 players were reportedly unavailable, with Cristian Romero, Joao Palhinha, Dominic Solanke, and Dejan Kulusevski all missing simultaneously through concussion protocols, knocks, and longer-term issues. The home record — two wins all season at the Tottenham Hotspur Stadium — tells the story of a squad whose relationship with its own fans has curdled into something toxic, while their paradoxically decent away form (double the home points) hints at a team that can only function when freed from the weight of expectation. The Champions League offered brief respite — a thrilling 3-2 second-leg win over Atletico Madrid — but ultimately ended in a 5-7 aggregate defeat, and now the sole focus is on seven games that will define whether one of English football's great institutions drops into the Championship.

The Optimal Path
In the best-case scenario, Tottenham's relegation risk drops to ~0% — effectively safe. But the probability of all those results landing together is roughly 0.0%. That's the ceiling.

Key Risks
Risk 1
high threat
Villa Park as a fortress is the most immediate threat. Unai Emery's side have won ten of their sixteen home league matches this season, accumulating 32 points at Villa Park — a venue where they have lost just four times. Tottenham's overall away record is their one lifeline this season, but Villa's structured 4-2-3-1 defensive shape, which compacts into a disciplined 4-4-2 medium block, is precisely the kind of organized resistance that has suffocated Spurs' attempts to play through the middle under Tudor's narrow 5-3-2. The reverse fixture at Tottenham in January ended 2-1 to Villa, so Emery already has a blueprint for dismantling this Spurs side.
Risk 2
medium threat
The dual threat of Ollie Watkins and Morgan Rogers — joint-top scorers for Villa with ten goals apiece in all competitions — poses a specific problem for Tudor's three centre-back system. When Spurs' wing-backs push forward to provide the sole width in their 5-3-2, the three centre-backs are left isolated in 1v1 situations on the flanks. Emery's 4-2-3-1 places wide attackers and overlapping full-backs in exactly those channels — Matty Cash and Lucas Digne are instructed to tuck in defensively but have license to exploit transitions, and with Watkins stretching the central defenders and Rogers drifting into half-spaces, the overloads could be devastating against a depleted Spurs backline.
Risk 3
medium threat
Emery's reputation as a tactical adjuster — a 'brilliant second-half team' coach who makes half-time interventions based on what he sees in the opening 45 — is a specific danger for a Tottenham side still learning Tudor's system. Tudor's aggressive man-oriented pressing demands enormous energy and coordination; even if Spurs execute it for 45 minutes, Emery's ability to shift Villa's build-up patterns after the break could systematically expose the gaps that appear when the press is bypassed. With Spurs having conceded 47 goals this season and Tudor's defensive record since arrival being dire, a second-half Villa surge is a high-probability scenario.
Where the Matchup Favours Tottenham
1
Counter-attack structure suits the away underdog
Tottenham have earned twenty of their thirty points on the road — exactly double their home tally — and the tactical reason is rooted in Tudor's 5-3-2 system. When Spurs play away, they naturally cede possession and sit deeper, which actually suits a formation designed to protect central areas with three centre-backs before releasing pace on the transition. Micky van de Ven's ability to carry the ball from deep, combined with the direct running of Mohammed Kudus and the creativity of Dejan Kulusevski (if fit), gives Spurs genuine weapons on the break. Villa's full-backs Cash and Digne tuck in to maintain compactness, but this very mechanism leaves wide channels vulnerable when transitions occur — exactly the spaces Spurs' wing-backs are designed to exploit. If Tudor embraces the away-day underdog role rather than trying to press Villa high, the structural mismatch between Villa's narrow defensive compactness and Spurs' wing-back width could yield chances.
2
Villa's midfield pivot weakened without Kamara
Boubacar Kamara's season-ending knee injury, confirmed in January, has removed Villa's most important defensive midfielder from the double pivot that underpins Emery's entire system. The screening role Kamara performed — sitting ahead of the back four, breaking up transitions, and allowing Youri Tielemans to push forward — now falls to alternatives like Ross Barkley or John McGinn, neither of whom offers the same defensive discipline. This creates a corridor through the centre of the pitch that Spurs' 5-3-2 is specifically designed to overload, with three central midfielders compressing play through the middle. If Lucas Bergvall and Joao Palhinha (assuming he clears concussion protocol) can dominate the central battle, Spurs could exploit the one area where Villa's structure has visibly deteriorated this season.
3
Corner defending improvement offers set-piece hope
One genuinely encouraging data point under Tudor is that Spurs have conceded just twice from corners in his tenure, compared to ten corner goals conceded across the full previous season. This suggests a tangible systemic change in how they defend dead-ball situations — critical when visiting a team with aerial threats like Watkins, who scored a header in the Europa League against Lille. More importantly for the attacking side, Spurs' narrow 5-3-2 naturally generates more wide play and crossing situations, and with Cristian Romero, Radu Drăgușin, and Van de Ven all standing over six feet, they have the physical profiles to threaten from attacking set pieces. Against a Villa side whose specific set-piece defensive system is unclear and whose overall defensive record has been described as 'noticeably worse' than last season, a well-delivered corner or free kick could be the route to a priceless away goal.
What to Watch For
Whether Tudor sets up to absorb Villa's first-half pressure in a deep 5-3-2 block and hit them on transitions — or whether he commits to his trademark aggressive man-oriented high press, which risks being suicidal against Emery's patient build-up through a Tielemans-led double pivot that is specifically designed to play through pressure.
Watch Spurs' wing-backs in the first twenty minutes: if they are pinned deep by Villa's wide attackers and full-backs, the 5-3-2 becomes a passive back five with no attacking outlet. If they're pushing high and committing to duels in Villa's half, it signals Tudor is gambling on an aggressive approach — and you should watch the space behind them for Villa's runners.
Monitor which Spurs players are actually available — with Romero, Palhinha, Solanke, Kulusevski, Drăgușin, and Gallagher all carrying recent injuries or illness. If Ben Davies is starting as the left centre-back in a back three, it signals the injury crisis is still acute, and the quality drop-off in the defensive line will be visible against Watkins and Rogers.

The Bottom Line
Tottenham's survival comes down to a brutal paradox: they need to win home games to stay up, but they are the worst home team in the Premier League with just two victories all season at the Tottenham Hotspur Stadium. Tudor's aggressive 5-3-2 pressing system hasn't had time to take hold — zero wins in five league matches — and the injury crisis stripping out Romero, Palhinha, Solanke, and Kulusevski simultaneously has left him trying to implement a high-energy tactical overhaul with a skeleton squad. The individual quality of Kudus, Van de Ven, and Kulusevski says this is not a relegation squad; the results say otherwise.

The Scenario to Build Towards
If Spurs can nick something at Villa Park, beat Leeds and Brighton at home to finally break the Tottenham Hotspur Stadium curse, and Arsenal and Brentford do the expected against West Ham in their respective fixtures — that combination could drop Tottenham's relegation probability into single digits and effectively secure survival with games to spare.
