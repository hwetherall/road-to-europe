The biggest pressure points are Tottenham’s own head-to-heads with fellow strugglers and lower-midtable sides: Chelsea away, Aston Villa away, Everton at home and Leeds at home all move the dial sharply, while West Ham’s run also matters because Spurs are trying to stay above somebody, not just accumulate points in isolation. In other words, Spurs do not need perfection. They need targeted damage.

Where the Matchup Favours Tottenham
1
Can Spurs break Chelsea's rest-defence shape?
Maresca-ball is built around controlled possession and a stable rest-defence behind the ball. For Tottenham, the opportunity is not to out-pass Chelsea for 90 minutes but to punish the moments when Chelsea’s full-backs and interiors vacate space and the first pass out beats the counter-press. If Spurs can turn recoveries into immediate vertical attacks, this stops the match becoming an endless territorial siege.
2
Second balls and the edge of the box
Chelsea’s structure often creates repeat entries rather than one clean chance and done. That means Tottenham’s box defending is only half the issue; the other half is who gathers clearances around the D and in the half-spaces. If Spurs keep losing those second phases, the shot volume will climb and the game will tilt hard toward Chelsea.
3
Set plays as an equaliser
When there is a talent gap or control gap, dead-ball situations can flatten it. Tottenham’s broader tactical discussion this season has included a greater emphasis on physicality and set-piece detail, and this is exactly the sort of fixture where one well-delivered corner or second-phase free kick can change the probability tree. Against a side that wants control, disruption matters.
What to Watch For
Whether Tottenham can escape Chelsea's first wave of pressure with one or two-pass exits instead of repeated clearances straight back to them.
The number of Chelsea regains in Tottenham's half, especially in the inside channels just outside the penalty area.
How many meaningful set-piece deliveries Spurs generate by half-time; if they are earning corners and free kicks, they are keeping the game alive.

The Bottom Line
The run-in is not asking Tottenham to become a different team; it is asking them to win the right games. That is the central lesson of the path search. The baseline says they are in trouble but not favourites to go down. The sensitivity says the danger is concentrated, not diffuse. Chelsea away and Villa away are the pressure-cooker fixtures because they can either create escape velocity or leave Spurs staring at the table. Leeds and Everton at home are where survival has to look like survival: direct, nervous, opportunistic, and ruthless enough to bank points rather than performances. There is also a very Premier League truth in the background: survival races are often decided by who can land one result that looked unlikely a week before. For Tottenham, that result is probably at Chelsea. The most plausible path in the search still allows them to lose at Villa, but it then demands a win at Stamford Bridge and a home win over Everton. That tells you the story. Spurs can absorb one setback, but not a chain of them. If they want to take control of their own narrative rather than scoreboard-watch West Ham, they need one upset and one home conversion. So the analyst's version is this: Tottenham do not need a miracle, but they do need a punch. Nick something on the road, finish the job in the six-pointers at home, and the numbers calm down fast. Miss those moments, and every other stadium suddenly matters too much.
The Scenario to Build Towards
The clearest threshold-crossing scenario is: lose at Aston Villa, beat Chelsea away, then beat Everton at home. That is Path 1 in the search, and it cuts relegation risk from 27.0% to 4.2%.
