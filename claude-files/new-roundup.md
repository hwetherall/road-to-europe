## 2. City Close the Gap, Spurs in Freefall, and the European Scrum Gets Brutal

## The Three Races

### Title Race

Bournemouth's win at the Emirates was the result of the weekend — and it cracked the title race open. Arsenal went into matchday 32 with a 98.6% chance of being crowned champions. They came out of it at 93.1%, a drop of 5.5 percentage points across the weekend. Manchester City, who dismantled Chelsea 3-0 at Stamford Bridge, are the direct beneficiaries: their title probability jumped from 1.4% to 6.9%, a gain of 5.5pp. That is still a long shot with six rounds remaining, but the gap has narrowed enough that it is no longer academic. Arsenal's average projected points fell by 2.2; City's rose by 1.4. One more slip and this conversation changes entirely.

---

### European Race

The European picture was reshaped not by the headline results but by a single draw that nobody saw coming. Brentford and Everton shared a 2-2 at the Gtech Community Stadium — Kiernan Dewsbury-Hall equalising in stoppage time to snatch a point — and the consequences rippled outward in every direction. Brentford's top-seven probability collapsed from 40.8% to 30.2%, a loss of 10.6pp, despite never falling behind. For Everton the damage was more modest — down from 35.4% to 33.2% — but the real transmission was felt by clubs not even on the pitch. Liverpool, who beat Fulham 2-0 earlier over the matchday on goals from Rio Ngumoha and Mohamed Salah, had their top-four probability surge from 16.1% to 29.1%, a gain of 13.1pp. The mechanism is straightforward: Brentford's stumble compressed the European places, pulling the cutoff downward and giving Liverpool — sitting just behind the top-four line — the breathing room their result deserved. Chelsea, meanwhile, lost at home to City and saw their top-four chances fall from 8.9% to 5.4% and their top-seven probability drop 11.6pp to 64.9%.

Further down the table, Brighton (+10.7pp to 33.9%), Sunderland (+10.5pp to 22.8%), and Bournemouth (+8.6pp to 12.8%) all made significant gains — Bournemouth's result at Arsenal the most eye-catching of the three. Newcastle's European prospects are covered in detail below, but the direction of travel is clear: they were swallowed from beneath across the matchday.

Manchester United's defeat at home to Leeds — more on that in the relegation section — cost them dearly in the European race too. Their top-four probability fell from 90.3% to 80.4%, the sharpest drop among the contenders, and a reminder that results at the wrong end of the table can detonate upward with alarming speed.

---

### Relegation Battle

Spurs and West Ham were the story. Sunderland's 1-0 win over Tottenham — Roberto De Zerbi's first game in charge ending in defeat — sent Spurs' survival probability plummeting from 65.6% to just 34.8%, a fall of 30.8 percentage points in ninety minutes. That is not a wobble; that is a structural shift. With six rounds remaining, Tottenham now sit below the survival line in the majority of projected outcomes.

West Ham provided the counter-narrative. Their 4-0 demolition of Wolves — a result that also lifted them out of the relegation zone for the first time this season — pushed their survival probability from 58.2% to 87.9%, a gain of 29.7pp. The gap between those two clubs in the survival odds has effectively inverted over the matchday. Leeds' win at Old Trafford — a result that barely registers in the top-four conversation but matters enormously at the bottom — lifted their own survival chances from 93.0% to 97.9%.

Nottingham Forest drew 1-1 with Aston Villa and saw their survival odds nudge down from 83.2% to 79.5%. They are not safe yet, and the ground between them and the bottom three is narrowing. The Spurs result, more than anything else this weekend, is the one that changes the shape of the final six games.

## 4. Mateta's Stoppage-Time Penalty Kills Newcastle's European Dream

## Crystal Palace 2–1 Newcastle

It took until the fourth minute of stoppage time, but Crystal Palace got the goal that mattered. Jean-Philippe Mateta stepped up from the spot and converted, snatching a 2–1 win at Selhurst Park that did serious, perhaps irreparable, damage to Newcastle's European ambitions. Newcastle had pulled one back — their goal came in the 43rd minute, briefly threatening a result that could have kept this season alive — but Glasner's side held their nerve when it mattered, and Mateta's penalty was the full stop on a game that Newcastle needed to win.

The shape of the match told a familiar story: Newcastle arrived with genuine intent, showed enough quality to stay in the contest, and then conceded the decisive moment at the worst possible time. A stoppage-time penalty is football's cruellest mechanism — you can do almost everything right and still lose. Newcastle did not do everything right, but this was a game they were in until the very end.

---

### Preview Callback

The Weekly Preview made several specific tactical calls about this fixture. Here's how they held up.

**1. Mateta as the central danger.**
The Preview identified Mateta as Palace's primary threat — physical, sharp in the area, and capable of holding the ball to bring teammates into play. It specifically noted his 84 shooting rating and flagged Glasner's use of early crosses to feed him. For 88 minutes, that threat was contained. Then Mateta stepped up and buried a penalty in stoppage time. The threat was correctly identified; the mechanism — a spot-kick rather than an open-play finish — was unexpected. The prediction landed, eventually, in the most damaging way possible.

**2. Gordon vs Sosa as Newcastle's repeatable advantage.**
The Preview pinpointed Anthony Gordon against Boso Sosa at left-back as Newcastle's clearest structural edge, arguing that Gordon's pace could force Glasner into positional adjustments and open central lanes. Newcastle's goal arrived in the 43rd minute, suggesting they were able to threaten in the first half — but the channel never became the sustained weapon the Preview envisaged. Partially correct: the logic was sound, the exploitation incomplete.

**3. Set pieces as a structural risk.**
The Preview explicitly flagged dead-ball situations as a danger, noting Palace's aerial threat through Mateta and their centre-back pairings, and warning that Newcastle's zonal defence needed to be disciplined. A stoppage-time penalty isn't a set-piece conceded from open delivery, but it does fall within the broader category of dead-ball jeopardy — and Newcastle's inability to see the game out without that late foul handed Palace the moment. The spirit of the warning was borne out, even if the specific mechanism differed. Partially correct.

**4. Wharton's distribution as the press trigger.**
The Preview argued that disrupting Adam Wharton's rhythm — forcing backward passes, letting Tonali step onto second balls — was key to cutting supply to Mateta. There is no evidence from the match report that Newcastle's midfield press successfully neutered Wharton for 90 minutes. Palace built patiently enough to earn the penalty and the result. This call did not land.

---

### Probability Impact

Before kick-off at Selhurst Park, Newcastle sat at **7.8%** for a top-seven finish — slim odds, but live ones. The defeat dropped that figure to **2.5%**, a swing of **5.3 percentage points** in the wrong direction.

The Preview had identified this fixture as the highest-leverage match of Newcastle's weekend, projecting a potential **+5.4pp** swing if the result went their way. It did not. The direction of the swing was right — this was always the match that would define the round — but the sign was reversed. With six rounds remaining, the arithmetic is brutal.

---

### Looking Ahead

Newcastle's next assignment will give us a clearer picture of whether this squad has the character to keep pushing, or whether Selhurst Park broke something. The margin for a European finish is now almost entirely out of their hands.
